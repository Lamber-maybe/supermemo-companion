# Why this exists

The full argument behind this skill's core principle — *internalize what
you think with, offload what you merely consult* — distilled from this
project's own README so it travels with the skill wherever it's installed.
`references/worth-learning.md` Altitude 0 gives the short version; this is
the long one, for when a user wants the case made in full.

## The argument

Since AI can now explain almost anything on demand, it is tempting to
conclude that learning is obsolete. That conclusion confuses two different
kinds of knowledge. Knowledge in the world is **inert** — it does nothing
until you query it. Knowledge in your head is **executable** — it runs
inside perception, comprehension, and judgment without being asked. A chess
master doesn't *recall* patterns; she *sees* the board differently. No
external oracle changes this, because the oracle sits on the wrong side of
your eyes.

Three things follow:

1. **You can't query what you can't name.** Good questions are made of
   internalized concepts. Whoever lacks the map of a domain cannot even ask
   about its territory.
2. **Generation is cheap now, so discrimination is the scarce skill.** AI
   produces fluent answers, including fluent wrong ones. Evaluating them
   requires internal anchors — mechanisms, orders of magnitude, failure
   modes. If everything is offloaded, you have no basis for doubt.
3. **Insight is collision.** Creative connections happen between pieces of
   knowledge resident in the same brain. Material you could look up but
   never internalized never participates.

The calculator is the template: it killed arithmetic drill but *raised* the
price of number sense. AI automates verbal-procedural work and raises the
price of domain sense. So the answer is not "learn less" — it is
**fewer items, deeper domains, better cards**. AI is a multiplier on what
you know, and a multiplier is worth more the more it has to multiply.

That's what this skill operationalizes: the AI serves the user's memory,
instead of replacing it.

## What should you learn, then?

Three rules of thumb for direction, before any list:

- **Deep beats broad.** AI rewards the person with the most to multiply;
  seventeen shallow hobbies multiply nothing.
- **Durable beats volatile.** Spaced repetition is a lifetime investment —
  buy assets that don't depreciate.
- **Loved beats dutiful.** Long learning runs on joy; a dutiful plan dies
  in three weeks. (This is also why the skill keeps the reading itself in
  the user's hands, and why it nudges — once, gently — toward
  self-formulation in the user's core domains.)

**Stop memorizing:** tool syntax and flags, API signatures, config formats,
exact values where an anchor suffices, step-by-step procedures always
performed with the tool open, framework-of-the-week details, isolated
trivia.

**Keep (or start) memorizing:** the vocabulary and ontology of the user's
working domains, mechanisms and causal models, X-vs-Y discriminations,
canonical examples and failure modes, orders of magnitude and base rates,
foreign languages (the purest latency case), and depth in their core
profession — details included, because fluency compounds.

## The four destinations for any piece of knowledge

| Bucket | Meaning | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| **MEMORIZE** | Needed to *think with*: at conversation speed, as a foundation, as a judgment anchor | 1–3 cards in the user's SRS |
| **MAP** | Needed to know it *exists*: what problem it solves, when it applies, where details live | exactly 1 "map card" |
| **OFFLOAD** | Merely needed to *consult*: retrievable in under a minute, verifiable on the spot | no cards; AI/reference at point of use |
| **DISCARD** | No plausible use, no connective value | nothing — deliberately |

## The seven tests

Applied to each knowledge unit when asking "is this worth memorizing?":

1. **Latency** — needed at the speed of thought, or is an under-a-minute
   lookup always acceptable?
2. **Foundation** — does the rest of the domain make sense without it?
3. **Map** — does knowing it exists change what the user can ask?
4. **Judgment** — will they use it to sanity-check AI or human output?
5. **Collision** — do they want it available for background thinking and
   creative connection?
6. **Half-life** — will it still be true in 5–10 years? (Buy assets that
   don't depreciate.)
7. **Desire** — do they simply want it to be part of them? Desire overrides
   every efficiency argument.

On top of the tests, one plainly economic check: anything used weekly or
more earns memorization outright.

Verdicts are **role-relative**: the same fact is MEMORIZE for the surgeon
and OFFLOAD for the patient. Latency and stakes decide, not the fact.

## The boundary: what this skill will and won't do

- It **will make cards from material the user supplies — after the
  gate.** Every unit is run through the worth-learning tests first; what
  passes gets carded by the formulation rules, and what fails gets a
  verdict and a reason. "Make cards from this" always includes deciding —
  and saying — what deserves no card.
- It will **not card around the gate.** No "twenty cards on X" where six
  earned existence: six cards and a list of reasons. An inflated deck
  isn't help; every card is a years-long stream of future review time,
  and the skill prices that in (workload notes) instead of celebrating
  deck size.
- It will **not summarize material as a substitute for reading it.**
  A summary the user didn't distill teaches almost nothing (the fluency
  illusion); this skill returns verdicts and cards, not digests, and the
  reading itself stays in the user's hands.
- It **still recommends self-formulation for core domains** — choosing
  what matters and wording it are themselves rehearsal, and incremental
  reading is *fun* — but the recommendation is one line, not a refusal.
  The user picks the mode; both are served in full.

## FAQ

**Does AI make spaced repetition obsolete?**
No — it makes it more valuable. AI is the best explainer anyone will ever
have, but understanding that isn't retained evaporates in weeks (the
fluency illusion). AI makes understanding cheap; SuperMemo makes it
permanent. They are complements: one produces, the other installs.

**Will it make the cards for me?**
Yes — gate first, then formulate. The units that pass the worth-learning
tests come back as properly formed Q&A and cloze cards in your tool's
import format; the units that fail come back as named skips with reasons,
because review time — not card generation — is the scarce resource. The
one-line nudge toward formulating core-domain cards yourself exists
because formulating is itself rehearsal; it is advice, never a gate.

**Can this be used with Anki or another SRS?**
Yes, first-class: the philosophy, the gatekeeping, and the card craft are
tool-agnostic, and `references/card-craft.md` speaks Anki's text-import
format (`{{c1::…}}` clozes included) alongside SuperMemo's Q&A format.
Only the tool coaching (`references/supermemo-coach.md`) and the
incremental-reading mechanics are SuperMemo-specific — Anki has no real
incremental reading.
